# Arriving Together: A Framework for Reader-First Writing

Psychologists refer to the curse of knowledge: once you
know something, you lose reliable access to what it felt like not to know it. The underlying
skill is holding two models in your head simultaneously: what you know, and what your reader
knows.

---

## The Four Elements of Good Contextualization

When reviewing or producing writing, check for all four:

**1. Announce the topic.**
Before saying anything about a thing, say what the thing is and why it matters right now.
"Here is a tool. Here is the problem it solves. Here is why you will need it." That sequence
is load-bearing, not padding.

**2. Establish stakes.**
A reader will not retain information they have no reason to care about. What breaks if they
don't know this? Stakes don't have to be dramatic, but they must be present.

**3. Signal structure.**
Small orientation phrases ("one gotcha," "three things to know") tell the reader what kind
of thing follows and where they stand in the argument. Use them.

**4. Earn transitions.**
Every paragraph should feel pulled into existence by what came before it. In conversation,
the listener can interrupt and ask. In writing there is no such escape valve.

---

## The Prior Question: Is the Gap Crossable?

Before applying the four elements, ask whether adequate context is even possible within
this piece. Sometimes a topic sits so far downstream from the reader's experience that the
context required would demand an earlier, different essay. The writer who senses this and
pushes forward anyway, piling in qualifications and parenthetical explanations, is not
contextualizing. They are procrastinating on a structural decision.

The graceful resolution is not the academic disclaimer ("beyond the scope of this paper"),
which is an apology. It is a confident pivot: name the boundary, gesture at what lies
beyond it, and return the reader to the work at hand.

---

## Principled vs. Evasive Omission

Hemingway: a writer can leave out anything they truly understand, and the absence
strengthens the work. McPhee called the underlying discipline selection: not
what is true or interesting in the abstract, but what the piece can hold.

---

## Review Questions

Work through these when reviewing or producing a draft:

1. Does the opening say what this is and why it matters, before the first substantive claim?
2. What breaks if the reader doesn't know this? Are those stakes visible?
3. Can the reader track where they are in the argument at all times?
4. Does each transition feel pulled forward by what came before it?
5. For each term, ask not whether the reader knows what it means, but whether they can be confident on what it means *here*.
6. When a point needs more context than the piece can hold, narrow it, reframe it, or let it go.
