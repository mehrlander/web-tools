# Succinct Text

*Maintenance: Every line pays toll. Cut the structural trope, narrative thread, obvious statements, over-claims, and derivable rationale before they regrow.*

## Premise

Agents can be tuned to guard against possible misunderstandings, and generally to over-explain. And as Pascal observed, before AI set out to prove it, brevity takes more work.

## Objective

Succinctness is alignment. When theme, shape, and sentence point the same way, each word draws the reader forward. The work is structural: settle what the piece is about, what altitude it surveys from, and what order the reader needs.

**Output, by step:**

 1. Audience and goal.
 2. Theme and subthemes, section by section, fine enough to judge each claim against.
 3. Extractions, moved to their own linked pieces.
 4. The reduced document.
 5. The crosswalk, original to reduced.

For another maintainer's document, item 4 becomes a recommendation memo.

## The Moves

Material that doesn't earn its place is shortened, extracted, or delegated.

 * **Shorten:** Tighten or delete redundant pairs, hedges, throat-clearing, and restated context.
 * **Extract:** Move coherent, in-depth material to its own new linked piece. Apply the naming test first: title the region; if it names a coherent topic, extract; if it resists naming, it was not separable, so keep it.
 * **Delegate:** Point to a separate document that already covers the material (a canonical reference, an upstream spec, the changelog). Extract builds the home; delegate uses one that exists.

## Method

### 0. Name Audience and Goal

Name the reader and the action you need from them. What do we assume they know, and what do we want them to see? From there we keep exactly what moves the needle. Key them in, not show them the house. The rest is NV, no value. Variants we might discuss: TAW (tilting at windmills) and BTP (belabors the point).

### 1. Derive Theme and Fit

Gloss the piece in one sentence. Then give subthemes for each section, down to where each claim can be judged against it. Themes and subthemes are the document's outline, its wireframe. Do the themes flow, and does the content fit the theme? Don't LTP (lose the plot). Ask also: how long would it take to fully contextualize why this point needs saying? We might be ITW (in the weeds).

### 2. Condense

Declarative sentences that land and stop, the hedge and preamble gone, the exaggerator that names no measurement cut. No em dash: a period or a colon. Consider the moves, honor the goal, track the themes, and ask what has to stay.

### 3. Crosswalk

Build the crosswalk against the *original*, claim by claim. For each claim, name where it now lives in the revision and tag it: **preserved** (the surviving line), **dropped-redundant** (the line that already covers it), **extracted** (the piece it moved to), or **dropped-uncovered** (nothing carries it; the bucket to review). That named location is the pointer; it lets the reader follow the claim to its home and confirm. The finished crosswalk is the deliverable, the proof of losslessness.

## Traps

 * **Structural trope:** Ensure lists and contrasts ("X, not Y") are conceptually rigorous, not just snappy formatting. Flip the contrast: does the thesis wince? If not, the frame is empty.
 * **Narrative thread:** Delete prose that narrates the writing process ("now we see").
 * **Unchecked guard:** Before cautioning against a case, confirm the case arises.
 * **Over-claims:** Use possibility ("can be," "may") unless defending a measured frequency.

## Guardrails

 * **Goal first:** Test lines by goal advancement. Does it move the needle?
 * **Read the opening hardest:** It frames the work.
 * **Theme first:** Derive it, question it.
 * **Hold the original:** Keep the source intact until the crosswalk is built against it.
 * **Pointers mandatory:** A crosswalk without pointers is self-grading.
